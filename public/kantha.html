About Kantha

Kantha is a centuries-old embroidery tradition from the Bengal region of the Indian subcontinent, encompassing present-day West Bengal, Odisha, and Bangladesh. This art form is celebrated for its simplicity, resourcefulness, and the vibrant stories it weaves into fabric.

What is Kantha?
Kantha refers both to the distinctive running stitch used and the finished textile product. Traditionally, Kantha embroidery was practiced by rural women, who repurposed old saris and dhotis by layering and stitching them together with simple running stitches, creating quilts, shawls, and covers. The word "kantha" itself means "rags" in Sanskrit, reflecting its origins in upcycling and sustainability.

  • Storytelling and Heritage: Each Kantha piece is unique, often depicting folk tales, mythological scenes, daily life, and personal stories through its motifs.
  • Generational Craft: Kantha was never a royal or commissioned art; it was passed down from mother to daughter, forming an important part of dowries and family heirlooms.
  • Symbolic Motifs: Common motifs include the lotus (purity), fish (fertility), peacocks (love), trees of life, animals, and scenes from rural life. These motifs often carry deep personal or spiritual meanings.
  • Technique: The hallmark of Kantha is the running stitch, sometimes combined with darning, satin, loop, and stem stitches for outlining motifs.
  • Materials: Traditionally, old cotton or silk saris and dhotis are layered and stitched together. The threads used are often pulled from the borders of these old garments, resulting in subdued colors like blue, red, black, and green.
  • Design Process: Designs may be drawn freehand or marked with chalk. Artisans create dense, intricate patterns that can take weeks or months to complete, depending on the size and complexity.

Kantha is not just embroidery—it's a living heritage, preserving the creativity, resilience, and stories of generations of Bengali women in every stitch.
