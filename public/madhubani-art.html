About Madhubani

Madhubani art, also known as Mithila painting, is a traditional art form that originated in the Mithila region of Bihar, India. This ancient art form has been passed down through generations of women, who created these intricate paintings on the walls of their homes during festivals and special occasions.

The art form is characterized by its vibrant colors, geometric patterns, and depictions of Hindu deities, nature, and social events. There are five distinct styles of Madhubani art:

  • Bharni: Filled with bright colors and intricate patterns
  • Kachni: Monochromatic line drawings with hatching
  • Tantrik: Spiritual and religious themes
  • Godna: Tattoo-style art with bold lines
  • Kohbar: Wedding chamber decorations

Each painting tells a story, preserving ancient folklore and cultural traditions through its symbolic imagery and vibrant expression.

Sacred Stories

The Tale of Sita's Exile

In Madhubani tradition, the story of Sita's exile is often depicted with intricate forest scenes, where every tree and animal carries symbolic meaning. The paintings show Sita's resilience through geometric patterns that represent strength and endurance.

Krishna's Divine Play

The mischievous tales of Lord Krishna are brought to life through vibrant blues and golds. From stealing butter to playing the flute, each scene is adorned with peacocks, lotuses, and celestial motifs that dance across the canvas.

The Cosmic Tree

The tree of life is a recurring motif in Madhubani art, representing the connection between earth and sky. Birds nest in its branches while fish swim in its roots, symbolizing the harmony of all creation.

Wedding Celebrations

Kohbar paintings traditionally decorated the wedding chamber, featuring symbols of fertility and prosperity. The bamboo grove, fish, and parrots all carried wishes for the couple's happy future together.
