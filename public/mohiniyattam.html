About Mohiniyattam

Mohiniyattam is a classical dance form from Kerala, known for its gentle, graceful, and undulating movements. It is characterized by its soft and swaying body movements, reminiscent of the gentle waves of the sea.
Origin: Believed to have originated in the 16th century, primarily performed by female dancers in temples and royal courts of Kerala. Its name comes from 'Mohini' (the celestial enchantress of Hindu mythology) and 'attam' (graceful, sensuous body movements).
Meaning: "Dance of the Enchantress."
Characteristics: Emphasizes fluid, circular movements, subtle facial expressions (sattvika abhinaya), and a distinctive 'lasya' (feminine) style. The movements are slow, deliberate, and lyrical, often depicting love and devotion.
Themes: Largely devotional, narrating stories of Hindu deities, especially Vishnu and Krishna, and often exploring themes of Sringara (love) and Bhakti (devotion).
Music: Performed to Carnatic music, with lyrics in Manipravalam (a blend of Sanskrit and Malayalam). Instruments include the Edakka (hourglass-shaped drum), Mridangam, Violin, Flute, and Kuzhitalam (cymbals).
Costumes & Jewelry: Dancers traditionally wear a white or off-white sari with a golden border (Kasavu), adorned with simple yet elegant gold jewelry, including a distinctive waist belt and hair bun decorated with jasmine.
Cultural Role: Historically performed by Devadasis, it faced decline but was revived in the 20th century, becoming a prominent classical dance form representing Kerala's cultural heritage.
Sustainability: Continues to be taught in various institutions across Kerala and beyond, ensuring its preservation and evolution.
Symbolism: The dance embodies grace, beauty, and devotion, often portraying the inner feelings and spiritual journey of a female devotee towards her beloved deity.

Enchanting Narratives

Sopana Sangeetham

Mohiniyattam often draws from Sopana Sangeetham, the traditional temple music of Kerala. Narratives from the Ashtapadis of Jayadeva's Gita Govinda, focusing on Radha and Krishna's love, are frequently performed.

Cholkettu and Jatiswaram

These are abstract dance pieces that showcase the technical prowess and rhythmic patterns of Mohiniyattam. While not narrative in themselves, they set the mood and display the pure dance elements (Nritta) of the form.

Padam and Varnam

These are expressive pieces (Nritya) that convey the emotional depth of the dance. Padams are short lyrical compositions, often depicting the longing of a nayika (heroine) for her beloved deity. Varnams are elaborate compositions combining Nritta and Nritya.

Dasavataram

The ten incarnations of Lord Vishnu are a popular theme, allowing the dancer to portray various characters and emotions through the fluid movements and subtle expressions characteristic of Mohiniyattam.

Krishnattam Influence

While distinct, Mohiniyattam shares thematic connections with Krishnattam, another dance-drama from Kerala, often drawing stories from the life of Lord Krishna, focusing on his playful and divine aspects.

The Story of Mohini

The very name of the dance form is linked to Mohini, the celestial enchantress avatar of Vishnu. Performances sometimes depict episodes where Mohini appears to deceive demons for the welfare of the gods, such as during the Samudra Manthan (churning of the ocean).
