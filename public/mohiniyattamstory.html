Mohiniyattam: The Dance of the Enchantress

Experience the gentle sway and lyrical beauty of Kerala's classical dance, embodying grace and devotion.

Enchanting Narratives

Sopana Sangeetham

Sopana Sangeetham, meaning "temple music of the steps" (referring to the temple steps), is the indigenous traditional music system of Kerala, deeply intertwined with temple rituals and the performance of indigenous arts like Mohiniyattam and Kathakali. Unlike the more structured Carnatic music, Sopana Sangeetham has a distinctive, flowing, and devotional character, often characterized by a slower tempo and a focus on evoking a particular emotional state.

The Devotional Narrative of Ashtapadis

Mohiniyattam frequently draws its lyrical content from the **Ashtapadis of Jayadeva's Gita Govinda**. This 12th-century Sanskrit lyrical drama describes the mystical and passionate love story of **Radha and Krishna**. Each Ashtapadi (meaning "eight-footed" or eight-stanza composition) explores a different emotional nuance of their divine love – Radha's longing, Krishna's playful dalliances, their separation, their jealousy, their arguments, and their joyous reunions.

The dancer, through the subtle and graceful movements of Mohiniyattam, interprets these verses. For instance, a dancer might depict Radha's anguish as she waits for Krishna, her eyes searching the horizon, her hands expressing her deep yearning. Or, she might portray Krishna's charm and his efforts to appease Radha. The narrative here isn't a linear plot but a series of emotional tableaux, each Ashtapadi a miniature "story" of love and devotion, allowing the dancer to delve into the **Shringara Rasa** (the sentiment of love) with great depth and delicacy. The Sopana style of rendering these verses lends a unique spiritual and contemplative atmosphere to the performance.

Influence and Atmosphere

The influence of Sopana Sangeetham ensures that Mohiniyattam performances retain a unique spiritual resonance. The gentle undulations of the music perfectly complement the flowing, circular movements of the dance. It creates an atmosphere of serenity and devotion, allowing the audience to immerse themselves in the emotional world of the narrative, whether it's the divine love of Radha-Krishna or other devotional themes.

Cholkettu and Jatiswaram

In Mohiniyattam, the **Cholkettu** and **Jatiswaram** are the foundational **Nritta (pure dance)** pieces that commence a traditional recital. While they do not tell a narrative "story" in the conventional sense, they are crucial in establishing the mood, showcasing the technical grammar of the dance form, and preparing both the dancer and the audience for the expressive pieces that follow.

Cholkettu: The Rhythmic Invocation

The **Cholkettu** is a vibrant and rhythmic opening item. It's an invocatory piece that typically features a short devotional verse (usually in praise of a deity like Ganesha or Devi) followed by intricate rhythmic syllables (**sollukattus or jatis**) recited by the vocalist and mirrored by the percussion (mridangam). The dance movements in Cholkettu are characterized by crisp, yet fluid, footwork and graceful arm movements. It acts as a kind of **"awakening"** – both for the dancer's body, gradually increasing its agility and responsiveness, and for the audience's senses, drawing them into the rhythmic world of Mohiniyattam. It establishes the basic stance, posture, and a sense of the 'lahari' or gentle sway unique to the form.

Jatiswaram: Melody Meets Rhythm

Following the Cholkettu, the **Jatiswaram** further explores the pure dance aspects. It's a harmonious blend of melodic phrases (**swaras**) from a chosen raga and intricate rhythmic patterns (**jatis**), presented without any lyrical content. The dancer performs complex sequences of **adavus** (basic dance units) and rhythmic footwork, synchronized precisely with the musical notes and percussion. The movements are fluid and undulating, emphasizing the typical **'attakara'** (swaying body movements) and **'cholkkettu'** (rhythmic patterns) of Mohiniyattam.

Jatiswaram is a pure celebration of abstract beauty. It highlights the dancer's command over **Laya** (rhythm) and **Tala** (meter), as well as their ability to execute precise and graceful movements. It’s an exercise in intricate geometric patterns formed by the body in space, showcasing the form's unique fluidity and lyrical quality without relying on storytelling. These pieces prepare the dancer for the emotional depth required in the expressive items by perfecting their technical foundation and bodily control.

Padam and Varnam

The **Padam** and **Varnam** are the expressive core of a Mohiniyattam recital, categorized as **Nritya** pieces. They move beyond abstract movement to convey deep emotional narratives and philosophical ideas, offering the dancer ample scope for nuanced **abhinaya** (facial expressions and hand gestures) and emotive portrayal.

Padam: The Heart of Emotional Expression

A **Padam** is a lyrical composition, usually short and slow-paced, that focuses intensely on conveying a specific **Bhava (emotional state)**, most commonly **Shringara Rasa (the sentiment of love)**. The narratives in Padams typically revolve around the feelings of a **nayika (heroine)** as she yearns for, longs for, is separated from, or rejoices in the presence of her **nayaka (hero)**, who is often a divine being like Lord Krishna or Shiva.

The dancer interprets each line of the poetry, often repeating it multiple times to elaborate on its various meanings and emotional nuances. This technique, known as **'sanchari bhava,'** allows for rich, detailed storytelling without a complex plot. For example, a Padam describing Radha's longing for Krishna might depict her restless sleep, her gaze fixed on the path he might take, her sighing, or her remembering past joyful moments. The movements are subtle, relying heavily on the dancer's eyes, eyebrows, and gentle facial expressions, along with precise hand gestures (mudras), to draw the audience into the character's emotional world. Padams are often moments of profound stillness and introspection in a performance.

Varnam: The Comprehensive Canvas

The **Varnam** is the **central and most elaborate item** in a Mohiniyattam recital, a comprehensive composition that seamlessly blends both **Nritta (pure dance)** and **Nritya (expressive dance)**. It can last for a considerable duration, allowing the dancer to demonstrate their complete mastery over all aspects of Mohiniyattam.

The narrative of a Varnam is typically a rich, single theme, often a devotional one, such as the praise of a particular deity, or a detailed exploration of the Nayika-Nayaka relationship from the Gita Govinda or other poetic texts. The pure dance sections feature intricate footwork, graceful turns, and the characteristic swaying movements, showcasing the dancer's technical virtuosity and rhythmic precision. These Nritta passages act as interludes, providing a dynamic contrast to the expressive sections.

The Nritya sections within a Varnam are where the deep emotional storytelling unfolds. The dancer uses extensive **abhinaya** to interpret the lyrical content, portraying various moods, situations, and characters within the overarching theme. A Varnam dedicated to Krishna might describe his birth, his mischievous childhood antics, his heroic deeds, and his captivating charm, with the dancer portraying each aspect with vivid expressions and precise gestures. The seamless transition between the abstract and the emotional makes the Varnam a pinnacle of the Mohiniyattam performance, demanding immense stamina, emotional depth, and technical excellence from the dancer.

Dasavataram

The **Dasavataram**, literally "Ten Incarnations," is a very popular and visually engaging theme in Mohiniyattam, as it provides a vast canvas for the dancer to portray a diverse range of characters, emotions, and mythological narratives through the graceful and fluid movements of the form. This item typically depicts the ten primary avatars of Lord Vishnu, who incarnates on Earth to restore cosmic order and dharma whenever evil prevails.

The Ten Divine Manifestations

Each avatar presents a distinct narrative and character, allowing the dancer to showcase their versatility:

  • Matsya (Fish): The story of Vishnu saving Manu and the Vedas from a great flood. The dancer's movements evoke the vastness of the ocean and the gentle guidance of the fish.
  • Kurma (Tortoise): Vishnu supporting the Mandara mountain during the Samudra Manthan (churning of the cosmic ocean). The dancer portrays the steadfastness and immense strength of the tortoise.
  • Varaha (Boar): Vishnu rescuing the Earth goddess Bhudevi from the demon Hiranyaksha. The portrayal includes powerful, ground-hugging movements and expressions of triumph.
  • Narasimha (Man-Lion): Vishnu manifesting from a pillar to slay the demon Hiranyakashipu, who was tormenting his devotee Prahlada. This avatar allows for dramatic and intense expressions, contrasting ferocity with divine protection.
  • Vamana (Dwarf): Vishnu appearing as a dwarf brahmin to humble the arrogant king Mahabali, tricking him into giving away three paces of land, which Vishnu then covers the entire universe in. The dancer portrays humility and then cosmic expanse.
  • Parashurama (Axe-wielding Rama): The warrior sage who cleansed the Earth of unrighteous Kshatriyas. Movements reflect martial prowess and determination.
  • Rama (Maryada Purushottam): The ideal king from the Ramayana, embodying righteousness and courage. The dancer portrays episodes like the abduction of Sita, the building of the bridge to Lanka, or the slaying of Ravana.
  • Balarama/Krishna: While sometimes Balarama (Krishna's elder brother), more commonly it's Krishna, depicting his playful childhood (butter theft, Kaliya Mardan) and his divine love (Ras Leela). This offers scope for joyful, mischievous, and deeply emotional expressions.
  • Buddha: Portraying the serene and enlightened prince Siddhartha Gautama, emphasizing peace and compassion.
  • Kalki: The future avatar who will appear at the end of Kali Yuga to destroy evil and restore righteousness. This is often portrayed with dynamic, powerful movements symbolizing impending destruction and renewal.

Expressive Scope

The Dasavataram provides rich opportunities for the Mohiniyattam dancer to utilize a wide spectrum of **abhinaya**. Each avatar requires different facial expressions, hand gestures, and body postures to convey their unique attributes and the events associated with them. The fluid, undulating movements of Mohiniyattam adapt beautifully to portray both the gentle grace of some avatars and the powerful actions of others, while maintaining the inherent lyrical quality of the dance. It's a visually spectacular and devotionally inspiring item that celebrates Vishnu's omnipresence and his role as the preserver of the universe.

Krishnattam Influence

While Mohiniyattam is primarily a solo dance form focused on feminine grace and lyrical expression, it shares deep thematic connections with **Krishnattam**, another significant classical performing art form from Kerala. Krishnattam is a vibrant, ritualistic temple art, a unique form of dance-drama that originated in the 17th century at the Guruvayoor Sree Krishna Temple. It exclusively narrates the entire saga of Lord Krishna's life, divided into eight plays.

Shared Devotion to Lord Krishna

The common thread between Mohiniyattam and Krishnattam is their profound devotion to **Lord Krishna**. Many of the themes, emotions, and specific episodes that are central to Krishnattam find their way into Mohiniyattam repertoire, particularly in **Padams** and **Varnams**. Mohiniyattam often draws from the rich poetic literature celebrating Krishna's life, including:

  • Childhood Leelas (Playful Antics): Portraying baby Krishna stealing butter, his mischievous pranks on the Gopis, or his enchanting flute playing that captivates all. The dancer uses joyful, innocent, and sometimes mischievous expressions.
  • Radha-Krishna Love Stories: The eternal love and separation of Radha and Krishna, as depicted in texts like Gita Govinda, are a mainstay. The dancer conveys longing, jealousy, joy, and reconciliation through subtle and profound abhinaya.
  • Heroic Deeds: While Mohiniyattam's grace might seem less suited for overt battles, it can subtly depict Krishna's bravery, such as lifting the Govardhan mountain or subduing the serpent Kaliya, focusing on the divine power and charm rather than raw physicality.

Emphasis on Bhakti and Shringara

Both forms prioritize **Bhakti Rasa (devotional sentiment)**, but Mohiniyattam particularly excels in exploring **Shringara Bhakti** – devotion expressed through the lens of love, often portraying the devotee as a yearning lover for the divine. The influence from Krishnattam reinforces Mohiniyattam's deep roots in Vaishnavite devotional traditions, allowing the dancer to embody the diverse facets of love, surrender, and spiritual longing for Lord Krishna. The narrative in Mohiniyattam, influenced by Krishnattam, is less about literal dramatic enactment and more about evoking the emotional experience of these beloved Krishna stories through the dancer's expressive interpretation.

The Story of Mohini

The very name of the dance form, **Mohiniyattam**, translates to "the dance of the enchantress" (Mohini: enchantress, Attam: dance). This name directly links the art form to **Mohini**, a celestial enchantress avatar of Lord Vishnu. The narratives involving Mohini are iconic and fascinating, allowing for a unique blend of grace, deception, and divine intervention. Mohini's appearances are always for the welfare of the gods and to subdue evil, often by trickery and irresistible charm.

Samudra Manthan (Churning of the Ocean)

One of the most famous episodes featuring Mohini is during the **Samudra Manthan** (the churning of the cosmic ocean of milk). Both Devas (gods) and Asuras (demons) collaborated to churn the ocean to obtain the elixir of immortality, **Amrita**. After immense effort, the Amrita finally emerged, but the Asuras immediately seized it, refusing to share it with the Devas. A fierce battle ensued for possession of the elixir.

To prevent the Amrita from falling into the hands of the demons, which would make them immortal and invincible, Lord Vishnu assumed the form of **Mohini**. She appeared before the Asuras as an exquisitely beautiful and alluring woman. Her charm was so overwhelming that the demons, captivated by her beauty, readily agreed to her suggestion that she distribute the Amrita fairly between them and the gods. Mohini, with her enchanting smile and graceful movements, then proceeded to serve the Amrita only to the Devas, cleverly denying it to the Asuras. By the time the demons realized her trick, the Devas had already consumed the elixir, gaining immortality. This scene is often depicted in Mohiniyattam, showcasing the dancer's ability to embody divine beauty, subtle deception, and graceful allure.

Mohini and Bhasmasura (Revisited in Mohiniyattam Context)

While also a popular Kuchipudi piece, the story of **Mohini and Bhasmasura** (as previously described) is also interpreted in Mohiniyattam. Here, the emphasis is less on the dramatic transformation and more on the fluid grace and seductive charm of Mohini that leads the demon Bhasmasura to his demise. The Mohiniyattam dancer would highlight the gentle, circular movements and the suggestive expressions of Mohini, creating an atmosphere of alluring danger, rather than sharp, distinct character shifts. The fluid nature of Mohiniyattam is perfectly suited to portraying the deceptive yet beautiful movements of the enchantress.

Symbolism

The "Story of Mohini" serves as a foundational narrative for the dance form itself. It symbolizes the power of divine beauty and feminine grace (Lasya) to overcome evil and restore balance. It encapsulates the essence of Mohiniyattam – a dance that is inherently beautiful, graceful, and capable of conveying profound mythological narratives with subtlety and charm.
