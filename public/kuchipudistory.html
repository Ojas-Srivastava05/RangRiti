Kuchipudi: Mythology in Motion

Witness the vibrant storytelling and fluid grace of Andhra Pradesh's captivating dance-drama.

Mythology in Motion

Bhama Kalapam

Bhama Kalapam is undoubtedly the most iconic and frequently performed Kuchipudi dance-drama, a masterpiece attributed to the revered Siddhendra Yogi from the 17th century. It narrates a charming and emotionally rich episode from the life of Satyabhama, Lord Krishna's proud and beautiful chief queen.

Satyabhama's Pride and Separation

The central character, Satyabhama, is depicted as a beautiful, wealthy, and somewhat arrogant queen, immensely confident in her charm and Krishna's love. The play opens with her expressing her unparalleled beauty and her profound love for Krishna, often bordering on possessiveness. A delightful and mischievous parrot, **Chitra Lekha**, acts as her confidante, witnessing her moods and aspirations. Through the parrot, Satyabhama sends a message of love and longing to Krishna, expressing her desire for his attention and his presence.

However, due to her pride and sometimes demanding nature, a rift occurs between Satyabhama and Krishna. Krishna, perhaps to teach her humility or simply due to his dalliances, distances himself. This separation plunges Satyabhama into deep anguish and repentance. Her initial arrogance gives way to profound sorrow and intense longing for her beloved. The depiction of her **viraha-tapa** (pangs of separation) is a hallmark of the play, allowing for extensive and nuanced **sanchari bhava** (elaborate interpretations of a single line of poetry through expressions).

Humbling and Reunion

In her despair, Satyabhama consults a Brahmin, **Madhava**, who advises her on the path to reconciliation. Madhava suggests that she humble herself before Krishna and offer a **"token of love"** (often symbolized by a precious jewel or her hair, which she offers as a sign of surrendering her pride). She writes a poignant letter of apology and longing to Krishna, begging for his return.

Moved by her genuine remorse and unwavering love, Krishna eventually returns to Satyabhama. Their reunion is depicted with immense joy and tenderness, highlighting the triumph of true love and humility over pride. The play often concludes with the joyous union of the divine couple, symbolizing the union of the individual soul (Jivatma) with the Supreme (Paramatma).

Significance in Kuchipudi

Bhama Kalapam is considered the quintessential Kuchipudi solo repertoire for female dancers. It provides immense scope for a dancer to showcase all facets of their artistry: **Nritta** (pure dance) through intricate footwork, **Nritya** (expressive dance) through elaborate hand gestures and facial expressions, and **Natya** (drama) through the portrayal of a complete character arc. Satyabhama's transition from pride to humility, her intense longing, and ultimate joy offer rich opportunities for profound emotional portrayal, making it a beloved and timeless piece.

Krishna Leela Tarangini

Krishna Leela Tarangini (Waves of Krishna's Playful Deeds) is a renowned Kuchipudi dance-drama, a magnus opus composed by the saintly poet **Narayana Teertha** in the 17th century. This extensive Sanskrit opera beautifully captures and vividly portrays various episodes from the enchanting life of Lord Krishna, from his miraculous birth to his divine childhood exploits and his romantic encounters with the Gopis.

Chronicle of Krishna's Life

The Tarangini is a collection of 12 cantos, each comprising numerous lyrical verses (tarangams) that narrate specific events in Krishna's life. It begins with the circumstances leading to Krishna's birth in Mathura, his miraculous transfer to Gokul, and his early childhood. The dance-drama then delves into the delightful and mischievous escapades of young Krishna: his playful stealing of butter, his subduing of the serpent Kaliya, his lifting of the Govardhan mountain to protect the villagers from Indra's wrath, and countless other divine "leelas" (plays).

A significant portion of the Tarangini is dedicated to the **Ras Leela**, depicting Krishna's divine love and playful interactions with the gopis (cowherd girls) of Vrindavan, especially Radha. These sections are rich in romantic and devotional sentiments, exploring themes of yearning, separation, and ultimate union with the divine.

The Tarangam and Plate Dance

A unique and highly captivating feature of Krishna Leela Tarangini, and Kuchipudi in general, is the **"Tarangam"** piece. In this segment, the dancer performs intricate rhythmic patterns while **balancing on the rim of a brass plate (simhasana)**. The dancer moves the plate with their feet to the accompanying music and rhythmic syllables, often carrying a pot of water on their head or lighting a lamp, showcasing incredible balance, grace, and technical virtuosity. This iconic 'plate dance' is visually stunning and symbolizes Krishna's playful and miraculous nature, often associated with his childhood antics.

Musical and Devotional Richness

The compositions in Tarangini are set to various ragas and talas, offering a wide musical palette. Narayana Teertha's poetry is deeply devotional and philosophical, imbuing the narratives with spiritual significance. Through the portrayal of Krishna's life, the dance-drama aims to evoke **Bhakti Rasa** (devotional sentiment) in the audience, drawing them into the divine presence of the Lord.

Krishna Leela Tarangini is celebrated for its lyrical beauty, rhythmic complexity, and its ability to transport the audience into the enchanting world of Lord Krishna, making it a cornerstone of the Kuchipudi repertoire.

Prahlada Charitam

Prahlada Charitam (The Story of Prahlada) is a powerful and morally profound Kuchipudi dance-drama derived from the ancient **Bhagavata Purana**. It recounts the iconic tale of the unwavering devotion of a young boy, Prahlada, to Lord Vishnu, despite facing relentless persecution and attempts on his life by his own demonic father, Hiranyakashipu. The narrative culminates in the dramatic appearance of Narasimha, Vishnu's ferocious man-lion avatar, to uphold dharma and protect his devotee.

Hiranyakashipu's Arrogance and Prahlada's Devotion

The story begins with the demon king Hiranyakashipu, who has acquired a powerful boon from Brahma that makes him virtually immortal: he cannot be killed by man or beast, indoors or outdoors, by day or night, on land or in sky, or by any weapon. Inflated by this boon, he declares himself supreme and demands that everyone worship only him. However, his own son, young Prahlada, is an ardent devotee of Lord Vishnu.

Prahlada's unwavering faith infuriates Hiranyakashipu. He tries various means to turn his son away from Vishnu-worship and eventually to kill him. These attempts include throwing him from a cliff, poisoning him, having him trampled by elephants, and even trying to burn him in a fire with his aunt Holika (who perishes instead). Through all these ordeals, Prahlada remains unharmed, protected by Vishnu's divine grace, constantly chanting Vishnu's name.

The Pillar and Narasimha's Manifestation

In a final fit of rage and disbelief, Hiranyakashipu challenges Prahlada, asking him where his Vishnu is. Prahlada calmly replies that Vishnu is omnipresent, in every atom, even in the pillar right beside them. Enraged, Hiranyakashipu strikes the pillar with his mace.

At that very instant, the pillar shatters, and from within emerges **Narasimha**, the terrifying man-lion avatar of Lord Vishnu. Narasimha is neither fully man nor beast, it is dusk (neither day nor night), he appears at the threshold (neither indoors nor outdoors), and he places Hiranyakashipu on his lap (neither on land nor in sky) and disembowels him with his sharp claws (not a weapon). Thus, all conditions of Brahma's boon are cleverly circumvented, and Hiranyakashipu is slain.

Significance in Kuchipudi

Prahlada Charitam is a powerful Kuchipudi production, often performed as a full-fledged dance-drama with multiple characters. It allows for a dramatic contrast between the arrogant and terrifying **Hiranyakashipu (often a male dancer in the 'Daru' or character introduction style)** and the innocent, unwavering devotion of **Prahlada (often portrayed by a young male or female dancer)**. The climactic appearance of Narasimha provides an opportunity for powerful and awe-inspiring theatricality, making it a visually and emotionally impactful narrative that highlights the triumph of devotion and divine justice.

Usha Parinayam

Usha Parinayam (The Marriage of Usha) is a captivating and romantic Kuchipudi dance-drama that narrates the charming love story of Princess Usha, the daughter of the mighty demon king Banasura, and Aniruddha, the grandson of Lord Krishna. This popular piece, drawn from the Bhagavata Purana, is celebrated for its blend of romance, fantasy, divine intervention, and dramatic conflict.

Usha's Dream and Chitralekha's Quest

The story begins with the beautiful Princess Usha, who falls deeply in love with a charming prince she sees in a dream. Waking up, she is consumed by longing for this unknown figure. Her wise and magical companion, **Chitralekha**, a talented artist and a sorceress, notices Usha's distress. To help her friend, Chitralekha draws portraits of various gods, celestial beings, and princes. When she draws Aniruddha, Krishna's grandson, Usha immediately recognizes him as the man from her dream.

Using her mystical powers, Chitralekha then transports Aniruddha, while he is asleep, from Dwarka to Usha's palace in Sonitapura. The lovers are overjoyed at their unexpected reunion and spend time together in secret.

Banasura's Wrath and Krishna's Intervention

Eventually, Banasura discovers Aniruddha's presence in his palace and, enraged by the secret affair, captures him using his powerful 'Naga Pasha' (serpent noose). Usha is heartbroken and distraught. News of Aniruddha's capture reaches Dwarka, leading to a massive conflict between Lord Krishna and Banasura.

Banasura is a great devotee of Lord Shiva and possesses a thousand arms, making him a formidable opponent. A fierce battle ensues between the Yadava army led by Krishna and Shiva's forces, who come to Banasura's aid. The conflict is depicted with dramatic flair and powerful movements in Kuchipudi. Lord Shiva even uses his special weapon, the 'Jvarasamhara,' to create a fever to subdue Krishna, but Krishna counters it with his 'Jvara-hara' to remove it.

Resolution and Marriage

Eventually, Lord Shiva intervenes and asks Krishna to spare Banasura's life, as Banasura is his staunch devotee. Krishna agrees but reduces Banasura's thousand arms to just two. With the conflict resolved, Aniruddha is freed. The dance-drama concludes with the joyous marriage of Usha and Aniruddha, celebrated with divine blessings and widespread rejoicing. This story highlights the power of love, the protection of the divine, and the resolution of conflict through divine will.

Significance in Kuchipudi

Usha Parinayam is a highly engaging Kuchipudi production, often performed as a group dance-drama. It provides ample scope for diverse characterizations, from the romantic Usha and Aniruddha to the powerful Banasura and the divine Krishna. The interplay of romance, fantasy (Chitralekha's magic), and action (the battle scenes) makes it a vibrant and compelling theatrical experience, showcasing both the lyrical and dramatic aspects of Kuchipudi.

Mohini Bhasmasura

Mohini Bhasmasura is a popular and dramatic solo item in Kuchipudi, based on an episode from Hindu mythology, primarily from the Puranas. It recounts the tale of the demon Bhasmasura and Lord Vishnu's enchanting female avatar, Mohini, who tricks the demon into causing his own demise. This piece is renowned for its contrast between the demonic and the divine, and for showcasing the dancer's versatility in portraying two distinct characters.

Bhasmasura's Boon and Tyranny

The story begins with the demon **Bhasmasura** performing severe penance to Lord Shiva, seeking immense power. Shiva, pleased with his devotion, grants him a dangerous boon: anyone whose head Bhasmasura touches with his hand will immediately turn into ashes. Intoxicated by this newfound power, Bhasmasura immediately tries to test the boon on Lord Shiva himself, intending to usurp his position. Shiva flees in terror, seeking refuge from the demon.

Mohini's Enchantment

The gods, witnessing Shiva's plight and Bhasmasura's destructive potential, appeal to Lord Vishnu for help. Vishnu, ever the preserver, agrees to intervene. He assumes the form of **Mohini**, an irresistibly beautiful and enchanting celestial maiden. Mohini appears before Bhasmasura, who is immediately captivated by her divine beauty and grace. He forgets his pursuit of Shiva and is completely enamored with her.

Mohini, with her alluring dance and seductive charm, engages Bhasmasura in a dance. She dances with unparalleled elegance and precision, subtly leading Bhasmasura to mimic her every move. The dance sequence in Kuchipudi is a highlight, as the dancer portrays both the enchanting Mohini and the clumsy, lust-struck Bhasmasura through swift transitions in characterization, body language, and expressions.

The Demise of Bhasmasura

As Mohini continues to dance, she gradually performs a gesture where she places her hand on her own head. Bhasmasura, completely entranced and meticulously imitating her every movement, places his hand on his own head as well. As soon as his hand touches his skull, the powerful boon takes effect, and Bhasmasura is instantly reduced to a pile of ashes.

With the demon's destruction, peace is restored, and Lord Shiva is saved. This story symbolizes the triumph of divine intelligence and righteousness over evil and arrogance, even when seemingly protected by formidable boons.

Significance in Kuchipudi

Mohini Bhasmasura is a favorite solo item, especially for female dancers, because it offers an incredible opportunity to showcase **'Sanchari Bhava'** and rapid character transformations. The dancer must convincingly switch between the graceful, alluring Mohini and the crude, foolish Bhasmasura, using distinct expressions, postures, and movements. It is a testament to the dancer's skill in both **Lasya** (feminine grace) and a hint of **Tandava** (masculine vigor), making it a challenging and visually fascinating piece.

Golla Kalapam

Golla Kalapam (The Dialogue of the Milkmaid) is a unique and deeply philosophical Kuchipudi dance-drama composed by **Bhagavata Kshetrayya** or sometimes attributed to Siddhendra Yogi, though the exact authorship is debated. It stands apart from many other dance-dramas as it doesn't present a linear mythological narrative of gods and demons. Instead, it uses the everyday life and simple observations of a milkmaid (**Gollabhama**) to delve into profound philosophical, spiritual, and social concepts.

The Philosophical Milkmaid

The central character is a milkmaid, seemingly an ordinary village woman going about her daily chores, such as selling milk, curd, or butter. However, as she interacts with customers or simply reflects on her life and surroundings, she engages in a sophisticated philosophical discourse. Through her seemingly mundane experiences, she raises fundamental questions about the nature of existence, the human condition, the soul (Atma), the Supreme Being (Paramatma), the purpose of life, and the illusions of the material world (Maya).

The text often incorporates elements of Vedanta, Advaitha philosophy, and Bhakti (devotion). The milkmaid uses metaphors from her daily life ‚Äì the milk and its transformation into curd and butter, the cow, the village ‚Äì to explain complex spiritual truths. For example, she might question the true nature of what she sells (is it just milk, or is it a part of the divine creation?).

Humor, Wit, and Social Commentary

Golla Kalapam is also known for its blend of serious philosophical content with a significant amount of **humor and wit**. The milkmaid's character is often lively, playful, and sharp-tongued, using everyday language and colloquialisms. She may engage in playful banter, sarcastic remarks, or even subtle social commentary, making the philosophical discussions relatable and engaging for the common person. The interactions can also touch upon caste dynamics or societal norms of the time.

Performance Style and Significance

The performance of Golla Kalapam typically involves extensive **Vachikabhinaya** (expressive acting through spoken word) in addition to **Angikabhinaya** (body movements and gestures). The dancer, portraying the milkmaid, delivers dialogues, sings, and uses a wide range of facial expressions and body language to convey both the mundane and the profound. It requires exceptional linguistic prowess, spontaneity, and a deep understanding of philosophy from the performer.

Golla Kalapam is a unique and intellectually stimulating Kuchipudi piece that demonstrates the dance form's capacity to transcend mere storytelling and engage in profound philosophical introspection, while still being entertaining and accessible. It embodies the rich tradition of Kuchipudi as a dance-drama that not only narrates myths but also explores universal truths through relatable human experiences.
