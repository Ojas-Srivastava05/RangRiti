About Kathakali

Kathakali is a highly stylized classical Indian dance-drama form that originated in Kerala, South India. It is renowned for its elaborate makeup, intricate costumes, and detailed gestures.
Origin: Developed in the 17th century, evolving from ancient temple and folk arts of Kerala.
Meaning: "Katha" means story or play, and "Kali" means performance or art. Thus, Kathakali means "story-play".
Characteristics: Features towering headgear, painted faces (Chutti), expressive mudras (hand gestures), and dramatic facial expressions (Navarasas). Performances are long, often lasting all night, and tell stories through a combination of dance, music, and acting.
Themes: Primarily based on Hindu epics like the Mahabharata, Ramayana, and ancient Puranas, depicting divine, heroic, and demonic characters.
Music: Accompanied by traditional Kerala percussion instruments like Chenda, Maddalam, and Ilathalam (cymbals), along with vocalists singing in Sopanam style.
Costumes & Makeup: Characters are categorized by their makeup colors (Pacha for noble, Kathi for villainous, Kari for forest dwellers, Minukku for female/sages), which are meticulously applied over several hours.
Cultural Role: Traditionally performed in temple premises and noble households, it is now a global art form, preserving ancient narratives and artistic traditions.
Sustainability: Taught in gurukulams (traditional schools) where students undergo rigorous training for years to master the art form.
Symbolism: Each aspect, from makeup to gesture, is symbolic, conveying complex emotions and narratives without spoken dialogue.

Epic Narratives

Nalacharitam

A celebrated Kathakali play, it depicts the poignant love story of King Nala and Damayanti, their separation, and eventual reunion, filled with divine interventions and human emotions.

Kalyanasougandhikam

This popular play focuses on Bhima's journey to find the mythical Saugandhika flower for Draupadi, encountering Hanuman along the way. It showcases humor, strength, and devotion.

Duryodhana Vadham

A dramatic and intense play from the Mahabharata, depicting the final battle between Bhima and Duryodhana, culminating in Duryodhana's defeat and death, highlighting themes of justice and retribution.

Kiratham

This play narrates the story of Arjuna's penance to obtain the Pasupatastra from Lord Shiva, who appears disguised as a hunter (Kirata). It culminates in a fierce battle and Shiva revealing his true form.

Dakshayagam

Based on the Shiva Purana, this tells the story of Daksha's sacrifice, Sati's self-immolation, and Shiva's destructive dance (Raudra Tandava), leading to the creation of the Shakti Peethas.

Subhadraharanam

An enchanting play depicting the abduction of Subhadra by Arjuna, with the assistance of Lord Krishna. It's a romantic narrative filled with strategic planning and divine blessings.
