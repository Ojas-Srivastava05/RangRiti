About Bharatnatyam

Bharatnatyam is a major form of Indian classical dance that originated in the temples of Tamil Nadu. It is one of the oldest classical dance traditions in India, known for its sculptural poses and intricate footwork.
Origin: Traces its roots to the ancient Hindu text of Natya Shastra. It was traditionally performed by Devadasis in Hindu temples of South India.
Meaning: The name is derived from 'Bha' for Bhava (expression), 'Ra' for Raga (melody), 'Ta' for Tala (rhythm), and 'Natyam' for dance.
Characteristics: Characterized by fixed torso, bent knees (Aramandi), intricate footwork (adavus), and a sophisticated vocabulary of hand gestures (mudras) and facial expressions (abhinaya). It is known for its geometric precision and linear movements.
Themes: Predominantly devotional, narrating stories from Hindu mythology, particularly those related to Shiva, Vishnu, and various goddesses, often expressing spiritual and philosophical ideas.
Music: Performed to Carnatic music, accompanied by a vocalist, mridangam (drum), nattuvangam (cymbals), flute, and violin. The 'Nattuvanar' (conductor) guides the performance.
Costumes & Jewelry: Dancers wear a specially stitched sari, often in vibrant colors like red, orange, and blue, adorned with extensive temple jewelry including a headpiece (talai-samana), earrings, necklaces, and armlets.
Cultural Role: Historically a sacred temple art, it was revived in the 20th century and is now a globally recognized classical dance, symbolizing South Indian cultural heritage.
Sustainability: Taught through a rigorous Guru-shishya parampara (teacher-disciple tradition), ensuring the preservation of its classical purity and technical intricacies.
Symbolism: Every movement, gesture, and expression in Bharatnatyam is symbolic, conveying complex narratives, emotions, and spiritual concepts, transforming the dancer into a living sculpture.

Ancient Tales in Motion

Alarippu

A pure dance (Nritta) piece that begins a Bharatnatyam performance. It is a rhythmic invocation, a blossoming of the body, preparing the dancer and audience for the performance to come.

Jatiswaram

Another Nritta piece, Jatiswaram combines intricate rhythmic patterns (jatis) with melodic phrases (swaras). It showcases the dancer's mastery over rhythm and pure movement without explicit storytelling.

Shabdam

This is the first expressive (Nritya) item, typically a short piece praising a deity or king. It introduces simple dramatic elements and facial expressions, bridging pure dance and storytelling.

Varnam

The central and most complex item in a Bharatnatyam recital, Varnam combines Nritta and Nritya. It explores a single theme or story in depth, allowing the dancer to showcase both technical virtuosity and emotional expression.

Padam

A purely expressive piece (Nritya) that focuses on conveying a specific emotion or mood (Bhava). Padams are usually slow and lyrical, allowing the dancer to delve into the nuances of love, longing, or devotion.

Thillana

The concluding pure dance (Nritta) item, Thillana is a fast-paced and exhilarating piece. It features intricate footwork and dynamic movements, serving as a grand finale to the performance.
