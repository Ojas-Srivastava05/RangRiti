About Odissi

Odissi is one of the oldest surviving classical dance forms of India, originating from the temples of Odisha. It is characterized by its unique 'Tribhanga' (three-bend) posture and graceful movements.
Origin: Rooted in ancient Hindu temples, particularly those dedicated to Lord Jagannath in Puri, and performed by 'Maharis' (temple dancers) and 'Gotipuas' (boy dancers).
Meaning: Named after its place of origin, Odisha.
Characteristics: Emphasizes fluid torso movements, intricate footwork, and expressive hand gestures (mudras). The Tribhanga pose, where the body is deflected at the neck, torso, and knee, is central to its aesthetic.
Themes: Primarily devotional, focusing on the worship of Lord Jagannath (an incarnation of Vishnu) and stories from the Bhagavad Purana, particularly those depicting the love of Radha and Krishna.
Music: Performed to Odissi music, a distinct genre of Indian classical music, using instruments like the Mardala (percussion), flute, sitar, and violin.
Costumes & Jewelry: Dancers wear vibrant silk saris, often in bright colors like red, orange, and blue, adorned with elaborate silver filigree jewelry unique to Odisha.
Cultural Role: Historically a temple dance, it has evolved into a sophisticated classical art form, preserving the spiritual and artistic heritage of Odisha.
Sustainability: Continuously taught and performed globally, maintaining its traditional purity while adapting to contemporary stages.
Symbolism: The dance is a moving sculpture, drawing inspiration from the sensuous carvings of the Konark Sun Temple and other ancient temples of Odisha, embodying devotion and lyrical beauty.

Mythological Tales

Gita Govinda

Jayadeva's lyrical poem, Gita Govinda, is a cornerstone of Odissi repertoire. It beautifully describes the divine love play between Radha and Krishna, their separation, longing, and reunion, filled with rich poetic and emotional content.

Dashavatara

This popular item depicts the ten incarnations of Lord Vishnu (Matsya, Kurma, Varaha, Narasimha, Vamana, Parashurama, Rama, Balarama/Krishna, Buddha, Kalki). Each avatar is portrayed through distinct movements and expressions, showcasing Vishnu's cosmic role.

Battles of Durga

Odissi often features powerful portrayals of Goddess Durga's victories over various demons, particularly Mahishasura. These pieces highlight her strength, divine fury, and triumph of good over evil, often with dynamic and energetic movements.

Krishna Leela

Similar to other classical dances, Odissi includes various episodes from Krishna's life, from his childhood pranks (Bala Leela) to his youthful escapades with the Gopis. These are often imbued with a sense of playfulness and devotion.

Ardhanariswara

This unique thematic presentation depicts the composite form of Lord Shiva and Goddess Parvati, symbolizing the union of masculine and feminine energies (Purusha and Prakriti). It allows the dancer to explore both vigorous (Tandava) and graceful (Lasya) aspects.

Shiva Stuti

Devotional compositions praising Lord Shiva are common. These pieces often depict Shiva's cosmic dance, his attributes, and his role as the destroyer and regenerator, showcasing both his serene and fierce forms.
